INGLÉS STORY H5N1 avian influenza usually causes severe respiratory disease. But some human cases show conjunctivitis or keratoconjunctivitis first, or mainly in the eyes. This is important when people work near sick or dead birds or dirty bird areas. Ocular presentations: Reports from Hong Kong and other parts of Asia describe unilateral or bilateral conjunctivitis with redness, tearing, foreign-body sensation, and mild pain. Some cases also have photophobia and superficial keratitis. Isolated or predominant eye disease: In some patients, eye redness and conjunctivitis happen without clear respiratory symptoms, or come before systemic and respiratory manifestations. Transmission route: Ocular mucosa may be a portal of entry. Viral RNA and viable virus are found in conjunctival swabs in humans and animals. Exposure history: Most human H5N1 infections follow close contact with sick or dead poultry, wild birds, or their secretions/excreta. In endemic or outbreak areas, conjunctivitis after high-risk bird exposure should raise concern for H5N1. Take conjunctival swabs and nasopharyngeal specimens for RT-PCR. Use eye protection and hand hygiene. In such patients, consider H5N1 and use full airborne and eye protection precautions. --- DIALOGUE Narrator: Today, a patient visits the clinic with a red, painful eye.
